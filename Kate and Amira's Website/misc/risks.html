Risks and Threats

A critical look at the social, ethical, and legal risks posed by AI-driven hiring tools, with a focus on New Zealand.

Overview

While AI recruitment tools offer real efficiency benefits, they also carry significant risks. This is especially for people from disadvantaged or minority groups. This section looks at four main risk areas we identified through our research: algorithmic bias, lack of transparency, insufficient legal protection in New Zealand, and privacy and data security.

1.Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination

One of the biggest risks with AI recruitment tools is algorithmic bias, where the system produces outcomes that unfairly disadvantage certain groups. Because these tools are trained on historical hiring data, they can learn and repeat the same biases that existed in past human decision-making. For example, if an organisation historically hired mostly men for technical roles, the algorithm may learn to favour male applicants, without anyone explicitly programming it to do so (American Bar Association, 2024).

Amazon scrapped its own internal hiring tool after discovering it was systematically downgrading applicants who had attended women-only universities or whose CVs contained the word "women's." This happened not because the tool was designed to discriminate, but because it had learned from ten years of data that reflected existing gender imbalances in the tech industry (American Bar Association, 2024).

Research suggests these systems can also disadvantage older workers, people with disabilities, and candidates who speak English as a second language (Paz, 2025). This is particularly concerning in New Zealand, which has a diverse population. A study that interviewed 39 HR professionals and AI developers found that bias comes from three places: the data the model is trained on, how the algorithm is designed, and how the organisation chooses to implement it (Soleimani et al., 2022). This means organisations can't just buy a tool and assume it will be fair.

2.Lack of Transparency and Automated Rejection at Scale

Another major risk is the lack of transparency in how decisions are made, combined with the sheer scale at which those decisions happen. These systems are often called "black boxes" because even the organisations using them may not fully understand how a particular outcome was produced (Mujtaba & Mahapatra, 2025). 73% of employers are currently using AI in hiring decisions, and some report the technology automatically rejects up to 50% of all applicants before any human ever reviews them (Tilo, 2026).

For job seekers this is a serious fairness problem. A candidate can be filtered out without getting any reason, and with no clear way to challenge the outcome. This is especially relevant for students and early-career applicants who may lack the specific keywords or formal experience that screening tools prioritise this meaning that capable people with genuine potential may never reach a human recruiter (Beyond Recruitment, 2025).

The New Zealand government's Public Service AI Framework acknowledges this issue, stating that people affected by AI-assisted decisions must be able to understand how those outcomes were determined. The fact that this had to be written into government guidance suggests that meaningful transparency isn't currently being achieved in practice.

3.Insufficient Legal Protection in New Zealand

Legal Gap

New Zealand currently has no dedicated legislation specifically regulating AI in employment. These tools are governed by existing laws like the Privacy Act 2020 and the Human Rights Act 1993, which weren't designed with automated decision-making in mind.

This means the pace of AI adoption in hiring is outrunning the legal protections available to job seekers (Simpson Grierson, 2024). The EMA (2024) notes that employers are still bound by existing human rights obligations even when using automated systems, but without AI-specific legislation, enforcing those obligations when harm is caused by an algorithm remains legally complex and mostly untested in New Zealand courts (EMA, 2024).

The New Zealand Cabinet has agreed to a light-touch, risk-based approach to AI regulation rather than introducing specific legislation (New Zealand Digital Government, 2025). While this approach is flexible, it also means the responsibility for ensuring fairness falls on individual organisations rather than being enforced by law, which is concerning given that many organisations lack the expertise or incentive to do proper fairness audits.

4.Privacy and Data Security

AI recruitment tools collect large amounts of personal data (CVs, video recordings, and voice analysis). This raises serious concerns about how that data is stored, who can access it, and how long it's kept (EMA, 2024). Video interview platforms in particular collect sensitive biometric data by analysing facial expressions and vocal tone to assess candidates.

The Office of the Privacy Commissioner has released an exposure draft of a Biometric Code of Practice in response to growing concerns in this area. This signals that current protections are not adequate for the type of data these tools collect (Simpson Grierson, 2024). It's an important development because it shows that regulators are starting to recognise the gaps in the existing legal framework.

Overall Reflection

Together, these four risks paint a concerning picture for New Zealand job seekers, particularly those from minority or disadvantaged backgrounds. These are not hypothetical problems. Amazon's gender-discriminatory tool, the scale of automated rejection, the absence of AI-specific legislation, and biometric data collection by video interview platforms are all documented realities.

What they have in common is that they are largely invisible to the people most affected: the candidates. When a human recruiter makes a biased decision, it can potentially be challenged. When an algorithm makes the same decision at scale with no explanation, there is very little recourse.
