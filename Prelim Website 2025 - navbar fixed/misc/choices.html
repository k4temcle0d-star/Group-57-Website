Technology Choices

The range of AI hiring tools available to organisations, the ethical issues each one raises, and recommendations for responsible adoption in New Zealand.

Overview

The rise of AI in recruitment has produced a wide range of tools that employers can choose to adopt, each with a different level of automation and human involvement. These choices have significant implications for both employers and job seekers. The decision of which tool to use is rarely straightforward.

The core question for organisations is not just which tool to use, but how much human oversight to keep in the process. Each step toward greater automation increases efficiency but reduces transparency and accountability. This raises an important question: do job seekers in New Zealand have any real recourse when an algorithm makes the wrong call?

1.Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS)

At the most basic level, organisations can use AI-driven applicant tracking systems that automatically parse and rank CVs based on keyword matching. Tools like Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, and Oracle Recruiting are widely used by large NZ employers. Woolworths (Woolworths and Fresh Choice) and Wesfarmers (Bunnings and Kmart) have AI screening built in as a default feature — meaning many organisations are using algorithmic filtering without having actively chosen to do so (Judge & Dunlop, 2026).

This passive adoption raises real ethical concerns. Organisations that haven't deliberately chosen to use AI may not have thought about the fairness implications, audited the tool's outputs, or told candidates their applications are being processed by an algorithm. The choice isn't always about which ATS to use — sometimes it's about whether to actually govern the one that's already running.

2.Conversational AI and Chat Interviews

Beyond CV screening, employers can use conversational AI platforms that conduct text-based chat interviews before any human interaction happens. Woolworths NZ's adoption of Sapia's platform is a local example — every retail applicant completes an automated chat interview that scores their responses before generating a shortlist. This system handles approximately one million applications per year across Australasia (NZ Herald, 2024).

The main ethical issues here are around consent, transparency, and accessibility. Candidates may not even realise they're being assessed by an algorithm rather than a person. Applicants who aren't strong written communicators, or who have disabilities that affect how they type, could easily be disadvantaged by a system that's judging the quality and phrasing of their text responses.

3.AI Video Interview Analysis

A more invasive option involves platforms like HireVue, which assess recorded candidate responses for tone, word choice, and facial expressions. These systems claim to predict job performance but have faced significant criticism for their lack of transparency and their potential to disadvantage people with disabilities, language differences, or neurodiverse communication styles (Raghavan et al., 2020).

Collecting facial expression and vocal tone data also raises serious privacy concerns. In New Zealand, the draft Biometric Code of Practice from the Office of the Privacy Commissioner signals that regulators are starting to address this, but the code is not yet in force (Simpson Grierson, 2024). So right now, organisations using video AI tools are operating in a legal grey area when it comes to biometric data collection.

4.Fully Autonomous AI Interviewing

At the most automated end is Amazon's recently launched Connect Talent platform, which conducts voice interviews entirely without human involvement, 24 hours a day. It generates recruiter notes and candidate evaluations with no human present during the interaction (Benzinga, 2026).

This is a fundamental shift — from AI helping human recruiters to AI completely replacing the initial human interaction. While it offers maximum efficiency, it raises the most serious questions about accountability. If a candidate is rejected at this stage, there is no human who was present and can explain why.

Ethical Dilemma

The efficiency gains of fully autonomous AI interviewing come at a cost that falls almost entirely on candidates — the loss of the chance to make a human impression, and no meaningful explanation if they're unsuccessful.

Recommendations

Based on our research, here are our recommendations for NZ organisations using or considering AI hiring tools:

  • Run regular bias audits. AI tools should be routinely checked for discriminatory outcomes across demographic groups. This aligns with obligations under the Human Rights Act 1993 (EMA, 2024).
  • Be transparent with candidates. Organisations should be upfront about when and how AI is involved in evaluating applications. The NZ Public Service AI Framework requires this for government agencies and it should be a basic standard for private sector hiring too (New Zealand Digital Government, 2025).
  • Keep humans in the loop. Fully autonomous hiring shouldn't be adopted without a clear process for human review — especially at the point of rejection. Research consistently shows human oversight is critical to fair hiring (Beyond Recruitment, 2025).
  • Use skill-based assessments. Practical tasks that test what a candidate can actually do are a fairer basis for selection than AI-scored chat interviews, especially for entry-level roles (Scitex, 2025).
  • Invest in AI literacy. HR staff need to understand the tools they're using, what their limitations are, and how to read their outputs critically rather than treating AI scores as objective truth.

The goal isn't to avoid AI in hiring altogether — it's to use it in a way that's genuinely fair to everyone applying. As students entering the workforce, this affects us directly, which is exactly why we chose to research it.
